Instructions (click to expand/collapse)

(WARNING: This HIT may contain adult content. Worker discretion is advised.)

Thanks for participating in this HIT!

Given a short dialogue between two or more people, a plausible summary of this dialogue and a commonsense knowledge connecting the dialogue with the summary, you are asked to generate an implausible summary that satisfies the following conditions:

  1. minimally differs from the original plausible one
  2. violates the given commonsense knowledge
  3. is an incorrect summary for the dialogue
  4. is still relevant for the given dialogue (something that could have possibly happened within the context of the dialogue, but did not happen)

Commonsense knowledge consists of implicit and commonly known facts about the everyday life such as "Lemons are sour" or "If someone is hungry, then they would like to eat.". It is a type of knowledge that nearly everyone knows intuitively and considers obvious, but is typically implicit which means it is rarely stated explicitly in text. For example, encyclopedic facts such as "Ottawa is the capital of Canada" are not considered commonsense knowledge because this information can be explicitly found online (e.g. Wikipedia) and not necessarily everyone knows it.

An example of a dialogue could be:

Dialogue

1. #Person1#: Kate, you never believe what's happened.
2. #Person2#: What do you mean?
3. #Person1#: Masha and Hero are getting divorced.
4. #Person2#: You are kidding. What happened?
5. #Person1#: Well, I don't really know, but I heard that they are having a separation for 2 months, and filed for divorce.
6. #Person2#: That's really surprising. I always thought they are well matched. What about the kids? Who get custody?
7. #Person1#: Masha, it seems quiet and makable, no quarrelling about who get the house and stock and then contesting the divorce with other details worked out.
8. #Person2#: That's the change from all the back stepping we usually hear about.

An example of a summary for this dialogue could be:

Summary

#Person1# tells Kate that Masha and Hero are getting a peaceful divorce. Kate feels surprised and asks about their kids.

An example of a commonsense knowledge connecting this dialogue and the summary could be:

it seems quiet and makable, no quarrelling about who get the house Implies peaceful divorce

For example, in the above summary, the highlighted phrase "a peaceful divorce" is not explicitly stated in the dialogue, but rather can be implied from the highlighted phrase "it seems quiet and makable, no quarrelling about who get the house and stock" in Line 7 of the dialogue. Note that this knowledge is contextual (i.e. depends on the context given as a dialogue). Commonsense knowledge can be either general like "Lemons are sour", or contextual like in the previous example, but it is always something implicit.

Your task is to minimally modify the summary (i.e. change only few necessary words) so that the new summary violates the given commonsense knowledge and therefore is an incorrect summary for this dialogue.

For example, in the above summary, the given commonsense knowledge is about implying a peaceful divorce, so we can simply modify the adjective "peaceful" to "violent" and we will get the following summary that violates the given commonsense knowledge and also does not correctly summarize the dialogue.

Implausible (Incorrect) Summary

#Person1# tells Kate that Masha and Hero are getting a violent divorce. Kate feels surprised and asks about their kids.

Minimal Change Rules

In this task, we are essentially looking for an implausible "summary" that is deceptively close (hard to differentiate from) to the original one, but nonetheless is incorrect and violates our everyday knowledge. So, please be creative, but also follow the rules below to achieve this goal:

  • You are allowed to change up to 5 words in the summary. Note that you can also alternatively swap the existing words in place instead of replacing them as long as the resulting summary satisfies the conditions 1-4 above.
  • You should make sure that the implausible summary is still relevant to the given dialogue. So, you are not allowed to arbitrarily change the words such that it is completely out of context. For example, in the above summary, if you replaced the word "divorce" with "dog", this can be said to satisfy the conditions, but it is now out of context because "dog" is nowhere mentioned in the dialogue which makes the resulting summary easy to differentiate from the original one. So, you should try to replace words with related words (e.g. antonyms etc.) or possibly other words from the dialogue that satisfies the conditions above.
  • You should avoid simply negating the word in the summary unless that is the only way to achieve the goal. The goal in this task is not to achieve the minimal difference, but to produce semantically very close summary that is however, incorrect. So, if you can creatively change 2 or 3 dependent words to achieve this, then go for it rather than changing one word such as adding/removing "not".
  • Your change should directly target the given commonsense knowledge such that, semantically, the resulting implausible summary differs from the original one with respect to this knowledge.

Knowledge Relations

Below we list the available relations with a brief description and an example. Each relationship is represented as A Relation B where A and B refer to phrases from the context.

Attributional Relations
  • HasProperty - A has B as a property; A can be described as B. e.g. trial HasProperty free of charge
  • CapableOf - Something that A can typically do is B. e.g. knife CapableOf cut
  • HasA - B belongs to A, either as an inherent part or due to a social construct of possession. e.g. bird HasA wing
  • HasSubEvent - A and B are events, and B happens as a subevent of A. e.g. eating HasSubEvent chewing
  • IsA - A is a subtype or a specific instance of B; every A is a B. e.g. car IsA vehicle
  • MannerOf - A is a specific way to do B. Similar to "Is A", but for verbs. e.g. auction MannerOf sale
  • DependsOn - A depends on B. e.g. postage DependsOn weight of the box
  • CreatedBy - A is created by B. e.g. space rockets CreatedBy scientists and engineers
Physical/Spatial Relations
  • UsedFor - A is used for B. The purpose of A is B. e.g. hammer UsedFor put nails in the wall
  • PartOf - A is part of B. e.g. word PartOf sentence
  • MadeOf - A is made up of B. e.g. crowd MadeOf people
  • AtLocation - A happens at location B, or B is a typical location for A. e.g. try clothes AtLocation changing rooms
  • LocatedNear - A and B are typically found near each other. e.g. salt shaker LocatedNear pepper shaker
Temporal Relations
  • IsAfter - A happens after B. e.g. PersonX lands on the ground IsAfter PersonX jumps off of the swing
  • IsBefore - A happense before B. e.g. PersonX calls the bank IsBefore PersonX asks for a car loan
  • HappensIn - A happens during B. e.g. gift exchange HappensIn Christmas party
  • IsSimultaneous - A and B happens at the same time. e.g. dancing IsSimultaneous music playing
  • HasPrerequisite - In order for A to happen, B needs to happen. e.g. dream HasPrerequisite sleep
Causal Relations
  • Causes - A causes B to happen. e.g. been late for my meetings Causes lost some important clients
  • Implies - A implies B. e.g. wet cloth Implies caught in rain
  • HinderedBy - A is less likely to happen because of B. e.g. PersonX gives PersonY a ride HinderedBy PersonX does not have a car
Social Relations
  • xIntent - Person in event A intends to do B. e.g. PersonX calls the police xIntent report crime
  • xReact - Person in event A reacts as in B. e.g. PersonX makes a compliment xReact feels good
  • xNeed - Person in event A needs to do B before doing A. e.g. PersonX calls the police xNeed dial 911
  • xWant - Person in event A wants to do B. e.g. PersonX calls the police xWant explain everything to police
  • xEffect - Event A will have the effect B on the Person in event A. e.g. PersonX calls the police xEffect starts to panic
  • oReact - Others will react to event A as B. e.g. PersonX calls the police oReact feel worried
  • oWant - Others will want to do B for A. e.g. PersonX calls the police oWant dispatch some officers
  • oEffect - Event A will have effect B on others. e.g. PersonX makes a compliment to PersonY oEffect PersonY will smile
  • MotivatedByGoal - Someone does A because they want result B. e.g. PersonX competes MotivatedByGoal PersonX wins
Comparative Relations
  • Antonym - A and B are opposites in some relevant way. e.g. a lot Antonym occasionally
  • Synonym - A and B have very similar meanings. e.g. blackout Synonym lights are out
  • SimilarTo - A is similar to B. e.g. mixer SimilarTo food processor
  • DistinctFrom - Something that is A is not B. e.g. stay home DistinctFrom go dancing
  • RelatedTo - A is related to B. e.g. refrigerator RelatedTo freezing
  • DefinedAs - A is defined as B. e.g. gym DefinedAs fine exercise facility
Other Relations
  • Other - A and B have some other relationship that is not listed here. In this case, please provide a description of the relationship in the "Other relation" field.

In addition, there are negative relations which are the opposite of the relations above. For example, A Not xWant B means that the person in event A does NOT want to do B.

Dos and Don'ts (click to expand/collpase)

Important notes to consider:

Note, there are many other considerations, but these are the most important.

  • Make sure the implausible summary is minimally different from the original summary! This typically means changing very few words or switching the words in place in the sentence. Please see Examples section for some potential examples of minimal perturbation.
  • Make sure the implausible summary violates the given commonsense knowledge. This means that your change should directly result in violation of the given commonsense knowledge for the given context (dialogue).
  • Make sure the implausible summary does NOT actually summarize the given dialogue. This means that your minimal change should produce a "summary" that is no longer a correct summary for the dialogue. If you have properly made a change that violates the given commonsense knowledge, this condition should be automatically satisfied.
  • Make sure the implausible summary is still relevant for the given dialogue. This means that your change should not introduce any new concepts that are significantly different from the topics mentioned in the dialogue.
  • Please, avoid simple negations unless that is the only way to violate the given commonsense knowledge Most of the time, simply negating the verb in the summary might do the job, but what we are looking are more nuanced changes. Please try to be creative, but at the same time produce something that is related to the context (dialogue) such that your implausible summary almost looks like a correct summary, but it is not!

Example 1

Dialogue

#Person1#: You're finally here! What took so long?
#Person2#: I got stuck in traffic again. There was a terrible traffic jam near the Carrefour intersection.
#Person1#: It's always rather congested down there during rush hour. Maybe you should try to find a different route to get home.
#Person2#: I don't think it can be avoided, to be honest.
#Person1#: perhaps it would be better if you started taking public transport system to work.
#Person2#: I think it's something that I'll have to consider. The public transport system is pretty good.
#Person1#: It would be better for the environment, too.
#Person2#: I know. I feel bad about how much my car is adding to the pollution problem in this city.
#Person1#: Taking the subway would be a lot less stressful than driving as well.
#Person2#: The only problem is that I'm going to really miss having the freedom that you have with a car.
#Person1#: Well, when it's nicer outside, you can start biking to work. That will give you just as much freedom as your car usually provides.
#Person2#: That's true. I could certainly use the exercise!
#Person1#: So, are you going to quit driving to work then?
#Person2#: Yes, it's not good for me or for the environment.
Summary

#Person2# decides to follow #Person1#'s suggestions on quitting driving to work and will try to use public transportations.

Commonsense Knowledge

Yes, it's not good for me or for the environment. Implies quitting driving to work and will try to use public transportations

Here we can simply swap the actions of quitting driving with using public transportation or replace verbs/nouns to create a contradiction.

Implausible Summary 1

#Person2# decides to follow #Person1#'s suggestions on quitting using public transportations and will try to drive to work.
Implausible Summary 2

#Person2# decides to follow #Person1#'s suggestions on starting driving to work and will try to use public transportations.
Implausible Summary 3

#Person2# decides to follow #Person1#'s suggestions on quitting driving to work and will try to use his car.

Example 2

Dialogue

#Person1#: Good coming. What can I do for you?
#Person2#: I'm in Room 309. I'm checking out today. Can I have my bill now?
#Person1#: Certainly. Please wait a moment. Here you are.
#Person2#: Thanks. Wait... What's this? The 30 dollar for?
#Person1#: Excuse me... The charge for your laundry service on Nov. 20th.
#Person2#: But I did't take any laundry service during my stay here. I think you have added someone else's.
#Person1#: Ummmm...Sorry, would you mind waiting a moment? We check it with the department concerned.
#Person2#: No. As long as we get this straightened out.
#Person1#: I'm very sorry. There has been a mistake. We'll correct the bill. Please take a look.
#Person2#: Okay, here you are.
#Person1#: Goodbye.
Summary

#Person2# finds #Person2# being mischarged. #Person1# corrects the bill and #Person2# pays for it.

Commonsense Knowledge

Okay, here you are. Implies #Person2# pays for it

Here the implication is that #Person2# pays for the bill and leaves. So, we can simply change this part.

Implausible Summary 1

#Person2# finds #Person2# being mischarged. #Person1# corrects the bill and #Person2# leaves without paying.
Implausible Summary 2

#Person2# finds #Person2# being mischarged. #Person1# corrects the bill and #Person2# pays without leaving.
Implausible Summary 3

#Person2# finds #Person2# being mischarged. #Person1# corrects the bill and #Person2# makes #Person1# pay for it.
